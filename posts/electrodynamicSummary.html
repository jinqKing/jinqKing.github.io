6.3.1. Problem 1: Prove the Mean Value Theorem for the 2D Laplace Equation:

Prove that for any harmonic function φ(x, y) that satisfies the 2D Laplace equation \(\nabla^2\phi\) = 0, the value of φ at any point is equal to the average of its values over a circle centered at that point.

\dotfill 平均值问题

  1. 天才做法

    To prove the Mean Value Theorem for the 2D Laplace equation, we proceed as follows:

    1. \textbf{Laplace Equation:} In two dimensions, the Laplace equation is given by:
    \begin{equation} \nabla^2 \phi(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial y^2} = 0 \end{equation}
    1. \textbf{Introduce Green's Function:} We use the Green's function \( G(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}_0) \), which satisfies:
    \begin{equation} \nabla^2 G(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}_0) = -\delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) \end{equation}

    In 2D, the Green's function is:

    \begin{equation} G(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \ln \frac{1}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0|} \end{equation}
    1. \textbf{Green's First Identity:} Applying Green's first identity, we have:
    \begin{equation} \int_V \left( \phi \nabla^2 G + G \nabla^2 \phi \right) dA = \oint_{\partial V} \left( \phi \frac{\partial G}{\partial n} - G \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n} \right) ds \end{equation}

    Since \( \nabla^2 \phi = 0 \), this simplifies to:

    \begin{equation} \int_V \phi \nabla^2 G \, dA = \oint_{\partial V} \left( \phi \frac{\partial G}{\partial n} \right) ds \end{equation}
    1. \textbf{Left Side Simplification:} Using \( \nabla^2 G = -\delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) \), the left side becomes \( \phi(\mathbf{r}_0) \).
    2. \textbf{Boundary Integral:} Since \( G = \frac{1}{2\pi} \ln \frac{1}{R} \) on the boundary, the normal derivative \( \frac{\partial G}{\partial n} = -\frac{1}{2\pi R} \), and the boundary integral becomes:
    \begin{equation} \oint_{\partial V} \phi \frac{\partial G}{\partial n} \, ds = -\frac{1}{2\pi R} \oint_{\partial V} \phi \, ds \end{equation}
    1. \textbf{Conclusion:} Thus, we find that:
    \begin{equation} \phi(\mathbf{r}_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi R} \oint_{\partial V} \phi \, ds \end{equation}

    This proves that the value of \( \phi \) at \( \mathbf{r}_0 \) is the average of its values on the circle.

6.3.2. Problem 2: Image Charge Problem for a Point Charge Inside a Spherical Conducting Shell:

Consider a conducting spherical shell with radius \(R\). A point charge \(q\) is placed inside the shell at a distance \(d\) from the center. Find the image charge configuration and determine the potential distribution inside the shell.

\dotfill 球内任意位置场强

  1. Setup: Consider a spherical conducting shell with radius R, and a point charge q located at a distance d from the center.
  2. Image Charge: To satisfy the boundary condition that the potential on the conducting shell’s surface is zero, we place an image charge q' = −q R d at a distance R2/d from the center, on the same axis as the real charge.
  3. Potential Distribution: The potential at a point r inside the shell is given by the superposition of the potentials due to the real and image charges:
\begin{equation*} \phi(\vec{r})=\frac{q}{|\vec{r}-\vec{r}_q|}+\frac{q'}{|\vec{r}-\vec{r}'_q|} \end{equation*}

where rq = (0, 0, d) and rq′ = (0, 0, R2/d).

  1. Result: This configuration ensures that the potential on the surface of the shell is zero and provides the correct

potential inside the shell.
