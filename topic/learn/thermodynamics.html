1.3. 2.5 The random walk problem   二项分布 easy

A man starts out from a lamppost in the middle of a street, taking steps of equal length \(l\). The probability is \(p\) that any one of his steps is to the right, and \(q = 1 - p\) that it is to the left. The man is so drunk that his behavior at any step shows no traces of memory of what he did at preceding steps. His steps are thus statistically independent. Suppose that the man has taken \(N\) steps.

(a) What is the probability \(P(n)\) that \(n\) of these steps are to the right and the remaining \(n' = (N-n)\) steps are to the left?

(b) What is the probability \(P'(m)\) that the displacement of the man from the lamppost is equal to \(ml\), where \(m=n-n'\) is an integer?

1.4. 2.14 Direct calculation of \(\overline{n}\) and \((\Delta n)^2\)   normal

Consider an ideal system of \(N\) identical spins The number \(n\) of magnetic moments which point in the up direction can then be in the form

\begin{equation*} \tag{i} n=u_1+u_1+\cdots+u_N \end{equation*}

when \(u_i = 1\) if the ith magnetic moment points up and \(u_i = 0\) if its points down. Use the expression (i) and the fact that the spins are statistically independent to establish the following results.

1.4.1. Prove \(\overline{n}=N\overline{u}\).

\paragraph{Proof}

\begin{gather*} n=\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}u_j \\ \boxed{\overline{n}} = \overline{\sum_{j=1}^{N}u_j}=\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\overline{u_j}=\boxed{N \overline{u}} \blacksquare \end{gather*}

1.4.2. Prove \(\overline{(\Delta n)^2}=N \overline{(\Delta u)^2}\).

\paragraph{Proof}

\begin{gather*} \Delta n= \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\Delta u_j \\ \begin{split} {(\Delta n)^2} &= \left( \sum\limits_{i=1}^{N}\Delta u_i \right)\times \left( \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\Delta u_j \right)\\ &= \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\Delta u_j^2+\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\sum\limits_{i=1,j\ne i}^{N}\Delta u_j \Delta u_{i} \end{split}\\ \begin{split} \boxed{\overline{(\Delta n)^2}} &= \text{E}\left[ \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\Delta u_j^2+\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\sum\limits_{i=1,j\ne i}^{N}\Delta u_j \Delta u_{i}\right] \\ &= \text{E}\left[ \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}\Delta u_j^2 \right] = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N} \overline{\Delta u_j^2} \\ &=\boxed{N \overline{(\Delta u)^2}} \end{split} \blacksquare \end{gather*}

\paragraph{解}\(E\left[\sum_{j=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1,j\ne i}^{N}\Delta u_j \Delta u_{i}\right]\) 固定一个（比如 j），将一个先求和 \(\sum_{j=1}^{N}\Delta u_j (\sum_{i=1,j\ne i}^{N} \Delta u_{i})\)，则可见 \(\sum_{i=1,j\ne i}^{N} \Delta u_{i} =0\)

\begin{gather*} \overline{(\Delta n)^2} =E((\sum u -N \bar{u})^2)\\ =E((\sum u)^2)+E((N\bar{u})^2)-E(2N\bar{u}\sum u)\\ =E(\sum u_j^2+\sum_{j\neq i} u_ju_{i})+N^2\overline{\bar{u}^2}-2N^2\bar{u}^2 \\ =E(\sum u_j^2)-N^2\bar{u}^2=N(\overline{u^2}-\bar{u}^2) =N \overline{(\Delta u)^2} \end{gather*}

1.5. 2.15 Density fluctuations in a gas   easy

Consider an ideal gas of \(N\) molecules which is in equilibrium within a container of volume \(V_0\). Denote by \(n\) the number of molecules located within any subvolume \(V\) of this container. The probability \(p\) that a given molecule is located within this subvolume \(V\) is then given by \(p = V/V_0\).

(a) What is the mean number of molecules located within \(V\)? Express your answer in terms of \(N, V_0\), and \(V\).

(b) Find the standard deviation \(\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n\) in the number of molecules located within the subvolume \(V\). Hence calculate \(\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n/\overline{n}\), expressing your answer in terms of \(N, V_0\), and \(V\).

(c) What does the answer to part (b) become when \(V \ll V_0\)?

(d) What value should the standard deviation An assume when \(V\to V_0\)? Does the answer to part (b) agree with this expectation?

SOLUTION

1.5.1. Express mean number of molecules in \(V\) in terms of \(N,V_0,V\).

\begin{gather*} \overline{n} =\sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}n\binom{N}{n}p^n(1-p)^{N-n} =np =\boxed{N \frac{V}{V_0}} \end{gather*}

1.5.2. Find standard deviation \(\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n\) and \(\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n/\overline{n}\)

\begin{gather*} D(n)=Npq=\boxed{N \frac{V}{V_0}(1-\frac{V}{V_0})} \\ {\frac{\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n}{\overline{n}}}=\frac{\sqrt{D(n)}}{\overline{n}}=\frac{\sqrt{Npq}}{Np} =\boxed{\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sqrt{\frac{V_0}{V}-1}} \end{gather*}

1.5.3. Find limit when \(V \ll V_0\) ?   代入

\begin{equation*} V \ll V_0 \Rightarrow \frac{V_0}{V} \gg 1 \Rightarrow \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sqrt{\frac{V_0}{V}-1}\rightarrow \infty \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n}{\overline{n}} \rightarrow \infty} \end{equation*}

\paragraph{注}凡远远大者，趋于无穷。

1.5.4. Find \(\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n\) when \(V\to V_0\)? Does the answer agree with this expectation?

\begin{equation*} V\rightarrow V_0 \Rightarrow \frac{V_0}{V} \to 1 \Rightarrow \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sqrt{\frac{V_0}{V}-1}\rightarrow 0 \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\underset{\sim}{\Delta}n}{\overline{n}}\to0} \end{equation*}

We expect it to be 0.

He knows, however, that the distance between the two marks is equally likely to lie anywhere between 99.8 and 100.2 cm, and that it certainly does not lie outside these limits. After repeating the operation 50 times, the man will indeed have laid off a mean distance of 50 meters. To estimate his total error, calculate the standard deviation of his measured distance.

\paragraph{Solution} Every time, the length is between 99.8 and 100.2 cm.

The max error is \(|99.8-100|=|100.2-100|=0.2\) If randomly choose from the range, we can derive the average of error due to its length being equally alike. \(\overline{\varepsilon}=0\), \(PDF(\varepsilon)=\frac{1}{0.4}\)

\begin{gather*} \overline{\varepsilon}=\int_{-0.2}^{0.2}\varepsilon P(\varepsilon)\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=\int_{-0.2}^{0.2}\frac{\varepsilon}{0.4}\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=\frac{\varepsilon^2}{0.2}\bigg|_{-0.2}^{0.2}=0 \\ \begin{aligned} \sigma^2(\varepsilon) & = \int_{-0.2}^{0.2}\varepsilon^2P(\varepsilon)\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=\int_{-0.2}^{0.2}\frac{\varepsilon^2}{0.4}\mathrm{d}\varepsilon\\ \sigma(\varepsilon) &= \sqrt{50\sigma^2}=0.82 \end{aligned} \end{gather*}

2.4. 4   normal 数学技巧

The probability \(W(n)\) that an event characterized by a probability \(p\) occurs \(n\) times in \(N\) trials was shown to be given by the binomial distribution

\begin{equation*} \tag{1} W(n)\:=\:\frac{N!}{n!(N\:-\:n)\:!}\:p^n(1\:-\:p)^{N-n} \end{equation*}

Consider a situation where the probability \(p\) is small \((p\ll1)\) and where one is interested in the case \(n\ll N\). (Note that if \(N\) is large, \(W(n)\) becomes very small if \(n\to N\) because of the smallness of the factor \(p^n\) when \(p\ll 1\) . Hence \(W(n)\) is indeed only appreciable when \(n\ll N\) . )Several approximations can then be made to reduce (1) to simpler form.

2.4.1. Using the result \(\ln(1-p)\approx-p\), show that \((1-p)^{N-n}\approx e^{-Np}\).

\paragraph{Proof}

\begin{gather*} x\rightarrow 1,\ln x\approx x-1 \\ 1-p\rightarrow 1,\ln 1-p \approx 1-p-1=-p \\ (1-p)^{N-n}=\mathrm{e}^{(N-n)\ln (1-p)}\approx \mathrm{e}^{(N-n)(-p)}\overset{n\ll N}{\approx} \mathrm{e}^{-Np} \quad\blacksquare \end{gather*}

\paragraph{注}要用对数，就用 e 把原本项放到指数位置上做变换——数学技巧

2.4.2. Show that \(N!/(N-n)!\approx N^{n}\)

\paragraph{Proof} \(p\ll 1,n\ll N\)

\begin{gather*} \frac{N!}{(N-n)!} =N(N-1)\cdots(N-(n-1))\approx N^n \quad\blacksquare \end{gather*}

2.4.3. Hence show that

(1) reduces to

\begin{equation*}\tag{2} W(n)\:=\:\frac{\lambda^{n}}{n!}\:e^{-\lambda} \end{equation*}

where \(\lambda\equiv Np\) is the mean number of events. The distribution (2) is called the “Poisson distribution". \paragraph{Proof}

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} W(n)&=\frac{N!}{n!(N-n)!}p^n(1-p)^{N-n}\\ &\overset{(a)}{\approx}\frac{N!}{n!(N-n)!}p^n \mathrm{e}^{-Np}\overset{(b)}{\approx}\frac{p^nN^n}{n!}\mathrm{e}^{-Np}\\ &\overset{\lambda:=Np}{=\!=\!=\!=} \frac{\lambda^n}{n!}\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda} \quad\blacksquare \end{split} \end{equation*}
